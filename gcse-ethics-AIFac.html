Introduction: The Ethics of Emerging Technologies

New technologies like Facial Recognition and Artificial Intelligence offer incredible potential to solve problems and improve our lives. However, they also bring complex ethical questions and challenges that society must grapple with.

Ethical considerations involve thinking about what is right and wrong, fair and unfair, and the potential impact of these technologies on individuals, groups, and society as a whole. It's not just about what technology *can* do, but what it *should* do, and under what conditions.

In these case studies, we will explore some of the key ethical issues surrounding FRT and AI. Hover over keywordsThis is an example tooltip! for definitions.

Case Study 1: Facial Recognition Technology (FRT)

What is FRT?

Facial Recognition Technology (FRT)Technology capable of identifying or verifying a person from a digital image or a video frame from a video source. uses biometric software to map facial features from a photograph or video. It compares this information with a database of known faces to find a match. FRT can be used for identificationDetermining who a person is (one-to-many matching against a database). (who is this person?) or verificationConfirming a person's claimed identity (one-to-one matching). (is this person who they claim to be?).

Common Uses: Unlocking smartphones, tagging people in social media photos, airport security checks, helping law enforcement identify suspects, retail stores identifying known shoplifters.

Task 1.1: FRT Uses - Quick Check (Max 2 points)

Which two of the following are common and established uses of Facial Recognition Technology?

Ethical Concerns of FRT

PrivacyThe right of individuals to keep their personal information from being disclosed. FRT can enable mass surveillance, eroding privacy.: Mass surveillance without consent, tracking individuals' movements and associations.

Bias and AccuracyFRT systems can be less accurate for certain demographic groups (e.g., based on race or gender) due to unrepresentative training data, leading to misidentification.: Systems may be less accurate for certain ethnic groups or genders, leading to misidentification and potential false accusations or denial of services.

Lack of Consent: Collection and use of facial data often happens without individuals' explicit knowledge or permission.

Misuse: Potential for use by authoritarian regimes for oppression, or by individuals for stalking or harassment.

Errors and Consequences: False positives (misidentifying an innocent person) or false negatives (failing to identify a target) can have serious real-world consequences.

Chilling Effect: Constant surveillance can discourage people from participating in protests or expressing dissenting opinions.

Task 1.2: FRT Scenario Analysis - Music Festival (Max 3 points)

A city police force plans to use live Facial Recognition Technology to scan crowds at a large music festival. The stated aim is to identify known pickpockets and individuals on a watchlist for public safety concerns. However, the system will scan everyone attending.

Discuss one ethical benefit and two ethical concerns of this use of FRT.

Guidance Points:

  • Potential Benefit: Enhanced public safety, deterrence of crime, quick identification of wanted individuals.
  • Potential Concerns: Mass surveillance of innocent people without consent, risk of misidentification (false positives) leading to wrongful stops/detention, data storage and security of collected facial data, potential for bias in the FRT system affecting certain groups more, chilling effect on attendees' freedom.

Case Study 2: Artificial Intelligence (AI) in Society

What is AI?

Artificial Intelligence (AI)The theory and development of computer systems able to perform tasks normally requiring human intelligence, such as visual perception, speech recognition, decision-making, and translation between languages. refers to computer systems that can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. This includes learning, problem-solving, decision-making, understanding language, and perceiving environments.

Common Applications: Voice assistants (Siri, Alexa), recommendation systems (Netflix, Amazon), chatbots, medical diagnosis tools, self-driving cars, fraud detection, AI-generated art and music.

  • Self-driving cars

Ethical Concerns of AI

Job DisplacementThe loss of jobs due to automation or technological advancements, where AI or robots can perform tasks previously done by humans.: Automation of tasks previously done by humans can lead to unemployment in certain sectors.

Algorithmic BiasWhen an AI system reflects the implicit values of the humans who created it or the data it was trained on, leading to unfair or discriminatory outcomes.: AI systems trained on biased data can perpetuate and even amplify existing societal biases, leading to unfair outcomes in areas like hiring, loan applications, or criminal justice.

Accountability & Responsibility: Determining who is responsible when an AI system makes a mistake or causes harm (e.g., an accident involving a self-driving car).

Privacy: AI often requires large datasets for training, raising concerns about how personal data is collected, used, and protected.

Transparency and Explainability (Black Box AI)The difficulty in understanding how complex AI models, like deep neural networks, arrive at their decisions, making them a 'black box'.: The decision-making processes of complex AI models can be opaque, making it hard to understand why a particular decision was made or to identify errors.

Misuse: Potential for AI to be used for malicious purposes, such as creating autonomous weapons, generating deepfakes for misinformation, or for widespread surveillance and social control.

Impact on Human Autonomy: Over-reliance on AI for decision-making could diminish human skills and critical thinking.

Task 2.2: AI Ethical Dilemma - Automated Hiring (Max 3 points)

A company uses an AI system to screen job applications and shortlist candidates for interviews. The AI was trained on historical hiring data from the company.

Identify three potential ethical issues with using this AI for hiring.

Guidance Points (Consider these):

  • Bias: If historical data reflects past biases (e.g., gender, race), the AI might learn and perpetuate these, unfairly disadvantaging certain groups.
  • Lack of Transparency: Candidates might not know why they were rejected if the AI's decision process is a "black box."
  • Accountability: Who is responsible if the AI makes a discriminatory decision – the developers, the company, or the AI itself?
  • Dehumanization: Reducing candidates to data points, potentially missing nuances a human reviewer might catch.
  • Accuracy/Errors: The AI might incorrectly filter out qualified candidates or shortlist unqualified ones.

Task 3: Exam Practice Questions

Apply your knowledge to these exam-style questions.

1. Describe one ethical issue associated with the use of facial recognition technology by law enforcement. [2 marks]

Mark Scheme (Example):

  • Issue: Risk of misidentification / false positives (1 mark). Consequence: Leading to wrongful arrest or suspicion of innocent individuals (1 mark).
  • OR Issue: Mass surveillance / invasion of privacy (1 mark). Consequence: Tracking citizens without their consent / chilling effect on public assembly (1 mark).
  • OR Issue: Algorithmic bias (1 mark). Consequence: Disproportionately affecting/misidentifying certain demographic groups (1 mark).

2. Explain how algorithmic bias in AI systems can lead to unfair outcomes. Give an example. [3 marks]

Mark Scheme:

  • Algorithmic bias occurs when an AI system produces results that are systematically prejudiced due to flawed assumptions in the machine learning process or biased training data. (1 mark for definition/cause)
  • This can lead to unfair/discriminatory outcomes because the AI's decisions reflect and may amplify existing societal biases. (1 mark for explaining unfair outcome link)
  • Example: An AI used for loan applications, trained on historical data showing fewer loans to a certain demographic, might unfairly deny loans to qualified applicants from that group. OR An AI for hiring trained on data from a male-dominated field might unfairly screen out female candidates. (1 mark for a valid example clearly linked to an unfair outcome).

Extension Activities

1. Deepfakes and Misinformation

DeepfakesSynthetic media in which a person in an existing image or video is replaced with someone else's likeness using AI, often for malicious purposes or misinformation. are AI-generated videos or images that can realistically depict someone saying or doing something they never did.

Research Task: What are the primary ethical and societal dangers posed by deepfake technology? How might they be used to spread misinformation or cause harm?

2. AI in Creative Arts

AI is increasingly used to generate art, music, and writing. This raises questions about authorship, copyright, and the role of human creativity.

Research Task: If an AI creates a piece of music, who owns the copyright – the AI, its programmer, or the user who prompted it? What are some arguments for and against AI-generated art being considered "true" art?

3. Regulating FRT

Governments and organizations worldwide are debating how to regulate the use of Facial Recognition Technology to balance its benefits with the risks to privacy and civil liberties.

Research Task: Find one example of a proposed or enacted regulation regarding FRT (e.g., in the EU, a specific US state, or a company's policy). What are its main aims or restrictions?
