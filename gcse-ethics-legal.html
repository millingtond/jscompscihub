Navigating the Digital Rulebook: Key UK Computing Legislation

OCR GCSE Computer Science (J277) - Component 1.6: Legal Concerns

Laws in the Digital World...

Introduction: Why Laws for Tech?

As technology becomes more integrated into our lives, laws are essential to protect individuals, businesses, and society from harm and unfair practices. These laws define what is acceptable and unacceptable behaviour in the digital realm, covering areas from how our personal information is handled to the protection of creative works and the security of computer systems.

In the UK, several key pieces of legislation are particularly relevant to computer science. We will focus on three main ones:

  • The Data Protection Act (DPA) 2018 (which incorporates the EU's GDPR)
  • The Computer Misuse Act 1990
  • The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

Understanding these acts is crucial for anyone using or developing technology. Hover over keywordsThis is an example tooltip! for definitions.

The Data Protection Act (DPA) 2018 / GDPR

Purpose and Principles

The Data Protection Act (DPA) 2018UK law governing the processing of personal data. It sets out principles for how organisations must handle individuals' information. (Often refers to DPA 2018 which incorporates GDPR). is the UK's implementation of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). It controls how your personal dataInformation relating to an identifiable living individual. Examples include name, address, email, medical records, online identifiers. is used by organisations, businesses, or the government.

Key Principles of Data Protection (summarised):

  • Lawfulness, fairness and transparency: Data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and in a transparent manner.
  • Purpose limitation: Data must be collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes.
  • Data minimisation: Data collected must be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary.
  • Accuracy: Data must be accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date.
  • Storage limitation: Data must be kept in a form which permits identification of data subjects for no longer than is necessary.
  • Integrity and confidentiality (security): Data must be processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security.
  • Accountability: The data controllerA person or organisation that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data. is responsible for, and must be able to demonstrate, compliance with these principles.

Individuals also have rights, such as the right to access their data, the right to have inaccuracies corrected, and the right to have data erased in certain circumstances.

The Computer Misuse Act (CMA) 1990

Purpose and Offences

The Computer Misuse Act (CMA) 1990UK law making it illegal to gain unauthorised access to computer systems, to computer material with intent to commit further offences, or to modify computer material without authorisation. Also covers making/supplying malware. was introduced to deal with the problem of hackingThe act of gaining unauthorised access to computer systems or networks. and other forms of computer misuse.

Main Offences under the CMA:

  • Section 1: Unauthorised access to computer material. This covers basic hacking, like guessing a password to log into someone else's account without permission, even if nothing is changed or stolen.
  • Section 2: Unauthorised access with intent to commit or facilitate commission of further offences. This is more serious, e.g., hacking into a system with the intention of stealing data or committing fraud.
  • Section 3: Unauthorised acts with intent to impair, or with recklessness as to impairing, operation of computer, etc. This covers actions like deleting or corrupting files, or introducing malwareMalicious software designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorized access to a computer system (e.g., viruses, worms, ransomware, spyware). (like viruses or worms) that damages or disrupts a system.
  • Section 3ZA: Unauthorised acts causing, or creating risk of, serious damage. This covers acts that endanger human welfare or national security.
  • Section 3A: Making, supplying or obtaining articles for use in offence under section 1 or 3. This makes it illegal to create, distribute, or possess tools (like malware creation kits or hacking software) intended for committing CMA offences.

The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act (CDPA) 1988

Protecting Creative Work

The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act (CDPA) 1988UK law protecting intellectual property rights for creators of original literary, artistic, musical, dramatic works, software, films, and sound recordings. gives creators of literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works, sound recordings, broadcasts, films, and typographical arrangements of published editions, the right to control the ways in which their material may be used.

CopyrightA legal right granting the creator of an original work exclusive rights to its use and distribution, usually for a limited time. is an automatic right; creators don't need to register it. It protects the expression of an idea, not the idea itself. This means you can't copy someone's software code, song, or book chapter without permission, but you can write your own software that does a similar thing, or write your own song about a similar topic.

What it covers: Software (code), website content, music, films, books, photographs, video games, etc.

Infringements include: Making copies without permission, sharing copyrighted material illegally (e.g., peer-to-peer file sharing of movies), using someone else's images or music in your own work without a license or permission.

Creative CommonsA licensing framework that allows creators to grant public permission to share and use their work on conditions of their choice, offering alternatives to traditional 'all rights reserved' copyright. is a licensing system that allows creators to let others use their work under certain conditions, offering more flexibility than traditional "all rights reserved" copyright.

Is the following action likely an infringement of the CDPA? (1 point per correct identification)

Scenario A: Using a picture you found on Google Images as your company logo without checking its license or getting permission.

Scenario B: Buying a music album and making a copy for your friend to listen to.

Scenario C: Writing a book review and quoting a short paragraph from the book for criticism, with attribution.

Real-World Application of These Laws

These laws have a daily impact on how we use technology:

  • Data Protection Act: When you sign up for a website, it must tell you how your data will be used (privacy policy) and get your consent for things like marketing emails. You have the right to see data companies hold on you. Companies must protect your data from breaches.
  • Computer Misuse Act: Trying to log into someone else's social media, email, or bank account without permission is illegal. Creating or spreading viruses, or DDoSing a website are also offences.
  • Copyright, Designs and Patents Act: You can't legally download movies from pirate sites or copy your friend's purchased software. Using images from the internet for your own commercial project without permission can lead to legal trouble. Software developers own the copyright to their code.

Legislation Myth Busters!

Myth 1: "If it's on the internet, it's free to use for anything."

Reality: Most content on the internet (images, text, music, videos) is protected by copyrightA legal right granting the creator of an original work exclusive rights to its use and distribution, usually for a limited time.. Just because you can access it doesn't mean you have the right to copy, distribute, or use it commercially without permission or a proper license (like Creative Commons).

Myth 2: "The Computer Misuse Act only applies to serious 'hackers' attacking big companies."

Reality: The CMA applies to any unauthorised accessGaining access to a computer system, program, or data without permission from the owner or a legitimate user. to computer material, even if it's just looking at someone's files without permission on a shared computer or guessing a friend's social media password. The intent and level of harm affect the severity of the offence, but the act itself can still be illegal.

Myth 3: "The Data Protection Act means companies can never share my data."

Reality: The DPA regulates *how* personal data is processed. Companies can share your data if they have a lawful basis to do so (e.g., you've given explicit consent, it's necessary for a contract, or there's a legal obligation). The key is that it must be done lawfully, fairly, transparently, and for specified purposes.

Task 4: Exam Practice Questions

Apply your knowledge to these exam-style questions.

1. A company stores customer data insecurely, leading to a data breach where personal details are stolen. Which UK law has the company most likely infringed? State one principle of this law they have violated. [2 marks]

Mark Scheme:

  • Law: Data Protection Act (DPA) 2018 / GDPR. (1 mark)
  • Principle violated: Integrity and confidentiality (security) / Security principle. OR Accountability (for not ensuring security). (1 mark)

2. An employee uses their colleague's login details, without permission, to access company sales figures they are not authorized to see. Identify the Act that makes this illegal and the specific type of offence committed. [2 marks]

Mark Scheme:

  • Act: Computer Misuse Act (CMA) 1990. (1 mark)
  • Offence: Unauthorised access to computer material (Section 1 offence). (1 mark)
  • (Allow: Could also be Section 2 if intent for further offence is implied, but Section 1 is primary for just access).

3. A musician finds their latest song being used in a popular online video without their permission. Which Act protects their work, and what right has been infringed? [2 marks]

Mark Scheme:

  • Act: Copyright, Designs and Patents Act (CDPA) 1988. (1 mark)
  • Right infringed: Copyright / Right to control copying or distribution / Right to control public performance or communication to the public. (1 mark)

Key Takeaways

  • Data Protection Act (DPA) 2018: Governs how personal data is collected, processed, stored, and shared. Emphasizes principles like lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity/confidentiality, and accountability. Gives individuals rights over their data.
  • Computer Misuse Act (CMA) 1990: Makes it illegal to gain unauthorised access to computer systems/material, access with intent to commit further crimes, make unauthorised modifications (e.g., deleting data, spreading malware), or make/supply tools for such misuse.
  • Copyright, Designs and Patents Act (CDPA) 1988: Protects intellectual propertyCreations of the mind, such as inventions; literary and artistic works; designs; and symbols, names and images used in commerce. Protected by patents, copyright and trademarks. like software, music, films, books, and images. Prevents unauthorised copying, distribution, or adaptation. CopyrightA legal right granting the creator of an original work exclusive rights to its use and distribution, usually for a limited time. is automatic.
  • These laws are crucial for ensuring privacy, security, and fair use of digital technologies and creative works.
