What are Structure Diagrams?

A structure diagramA diagram that shows how a system or program is broken down into smaller, hierarchical modules or sub-systems. (also known as a structure chart or hierarchy chart) is a design tool used in software development and system analysis. It visually represents the breakdown of a system into its major components or modulesA distinct part of a larger system or program that performs a specific function or set of related functions., and the hierarchical relationships between them.

Purpose:

  • To illustrate top-down designA design approach where a system is broken down from a high-level overview into progressively smaller and more detailed sub-systems or modules. by showing how a complex system is decomposed into simpler, manageable parts.
  • To show the overall architecture or organization of a program or system.
  • To help developers understand the different parts of a system and how they fit together.
  • To aid in planning and allocating development work.

Structure diagrams are a key part of decompositionBreaking a problem down into smaller, more manageable parts. A key principle in computational thinking and system design., a fundamental principle of computational thinking. They help us manage complexity by focusing on smaller pieces of a problem one at a time.

The diagrams typically consist of rectangular boxes representing modules, with lines connecting them to show that a higher-level module is composed of the lower-level modules connected to it. Hover over keywordsThis is an example tooltip! for definitions.

Interpreting & Creating Structure Diagrams

From Scenario to Diagram

When faced with a scenario, start by identifying the main overall system. Then, think about the major functions or parts that make up that system. These become your first level of modules. Each of these can then be further broken down if they are complex enough.

Example 1: Pac-Man Game (from PDF Task 4)

Consider the classic Pac-Man game. What would be the main modules in a structure diagram for it?

Example Pac-Man Structure:

  • Pac-Man Game
    • Game Initialization (Setup Level, Scores)
    • Player Control (Input Handling, Pac-Man Movement)
    • Ghost AI (Movement Logic for each ghost, Chase/Scatter modes)
    • Collision Detection (Pac-Man vs Ghosts, Pac-Man vs Pellets/Fruit)
    • Score Management (Update Score, Lives)
    • Game Display (Render Maze, Characters, UI)
    • Game State Management (Start, Playing, Game Over, Next Level)

Real-World Context

Structure diagrams, or the principles behind them, are widely used:

  • Software Development: Large applications are almost always built as a collection of modules, classes, or functions. Structure diagrams help plan this modular architecture.
  • Website Design: A site map is a type of structure diagram, showing the hierarchy of pages on a website.
  • Project Management: Work Breakdown Structures (WBS) decompose a project into smaller tasks and sub-tasks, similar to a structure diagram.
  • Organizational Charts: These show the hierarchical structure of a company, with departments and roles.
  • Database Design: Entity-Relationship Diagrams (ERDs) show the structure of a database, though they focus more on data relationships than functional decomposition.

Structure Diagram Myth Busters!

Myth 1: "Structure diagrams show the order in which tasks are performed."

Reality: False. Structure diagrams show the hierarchyThe arrangement of modules in a system from the most general (top) to the most specific (bottom), showing levels of decomposition. and decompositionBreaking a problem down into smaller, more manageable parts. A key principle in computational thinking and system design. of a system into its component parts (modules). They show *what* the parts are, not the sequence of execution. FlowchartsA diagram that represents an algorithm, workflow or process, showing the steps as boxes of various kinds, and their order by connecting them with arrows. or pseudocodeA plain language description of the steps in an algorithm or another system. Pseudocode often uses structural conventions of a normal programming language, but is intended for human reading rather than machine reading. are used to show the order of operations or logic flow.

Myth 2: "A module in a structure diagram can only be broken down one more level."

Reality: False. Modules can be decomposed into sub-modules, and those sub-modules can be further decomposed into even smaller sub-sub-modules, and so on, for as many levels as necessary to represent the system's complexity clearly.

Myth 3: "Structure diagrams are only useful for really big, complex software systems."

Reality: False. While they are essential for large systems, the principle of decomposition and visualizing structure is helpful even for smaller programs or tasks. It promotes clear thinking and organized design, regardless of scale.

2. Explain one benefit of using a structure diagram during the design phase of a software project. [2 marks]

Mark Scheme (any one benefit with explanation, 2 marks):

  • Helps to break down a complex problem/system into smaller, more manageable parts/modules (1 mark), making it easier to understand and develop (1 mark).
  • Clearly shows the hierarchy and relationships between different modules (1 mark), which aids in understanding the overall system architecture (1 mark).
  • Facilitates top-down design (1 mark), allowing developers to focus on high-level structure before diving into detailed implementation (1 mark).
  • Can help in allocating tasks to different developers or teams (1 mark), as modules can often be developed independently (1 mark).

  • They show the hierarchyThe arrangement of modules in a system from the most general (top) to the most specific (bottom), showing levels of decomposition. of these modules, illustrating top-down designA design approach where a system is broken down from a high-level overview into progressively smaller and more detailed sub-systems or modules. and decompositionBreaking a problem down into smaller, more manageable parts.
